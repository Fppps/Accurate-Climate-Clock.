Methodology & Variables

VAR 01

The Carbon Budget

The accurate model utilizes a fixed remaining carbon budget of 130 GtCO₂. This represents the estimated maximum volume of atmospheric carbon humanity can emit while retaining a 50% probability of limiting planetary warming to 1.5°C (2.7°F) above pre-industrial baselines.

VAR 02

Static Mathematical Offset

By dividing the 130 GtCO₂ budget by a continuous annual emission rate of 42.2 GtCO₂, we compute a strict remaining timeframe of approximately 1,125 days. Subtracting this from the official Climate Clock's historical baseline of 1,217.72 days yields a permanent 92.58-day offset. This is not an error; it is a deliberate removal of complex variables to isolate the raw mathematics of our current emissions.

1217.72 - (130 ÷ 42.2 × 365.24) = 92.58 Days
VAR 03

Trajectory Linked Synchronization

Instead of running independently, the Accurate Climate Clock is now fed directly by the standard Climate Clock. As the live API shifts the official deadline based on new data, our accurate clock moves with it—perfectly maintaining its pessimistic 92.58-day discrepancy.

VAR 04

Transparency vs. Dynamic Forecasting

Is our clock more accurate than the official one? Not necessarily. The official Climate Clock is backed by leading scientific institutions and incorporates highly robust, complex trajectory models that update periodically. Our "Accurate" model instead serves as a transparent, raw mathematical baseline. By applying a strict static division of recent 2024/2025 estimates, it bypasses complex forecasting to show exactly what happens if our current emission rate fails to decrease.

Methodology & Accuracy

Is the "Accurate" Clock Actually More Accurate?

Understanding the difference between dynamic scientific forecasting and transparent mathematical baselines.

When you view our dashboard, you will notice a discrepancy between the Official Climate Clock and our Accurate Climate Clock. This naturally raises the question: which one is right?

The short answer is: They are both mathematically correct, but they are measuring two different things.

The Official Climate Clock: Dynamic Forecasting

The official Climate Clock is an incredible piece of scientific communication. It is backed by leading climate scientists, the IPCC, and institutions like the Mercator Research Institute on Global Commons and Climate Change.

It uses dynamic trajectory models. This means it doesn't just look at what we are emitting today; it looks at complex forecasts. It attempts to factor in:

  • Fluctuations in the Earth's natural carbon sinks (like oceans and forests).
  • Complex variables regarding other greenhouse gases (methane, nitrous oxide).
  • Smoothing algorithms to account for gradual changes in global policies and emission reduction pledges.

Because of these complexities, the official clock provides a highly educated, scientifically smoothed forecast of when we will hit +1.5°C (+2.7°F) of warming.

Our Clock: The Strict Mathematical Baseline

Our "Accurate" clock takes a much more aggressive, entirely transparent approach. We strip away the complex forecasting models and rely on two undeniable data points based on recent estimates:

  • The Remaining Carbon Budget: 130 GtCO₂ (The maximum amount we can emit to keep a 50% chance of staying under +1.5°C / +2.7°F).
  • Current Annual Emissions: 42.2 GtCO₂ per year.

If you divide 130 by 42.2, you get roughly 3.08 years (about 1,125 days). By anchoring this math to the official clock, we establish a permanent 92.58-day offset.

So, why call it "Accurate"?

This 92.58-day gap isn't a "failed accuracy" or a deficit in calculation. Rather, we are intentionally taking away something complex to see what we are doing with the numbers instead of calculating what we are doing to the air, oceans, and Earth systems.

The official clock assumes some level of future trajectory shifting and natural planetary absorption. Our clock shows exactly what happens if we do absolutely nothing to slow down right now, looking strictly at human output versus the remaining budget.

In a sense, the official clock is the most accurate planetary prediction, while our clock is the most accurate reflection of present-day mathematical momentum.
